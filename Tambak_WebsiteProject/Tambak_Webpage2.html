Jane Austen

Jane Austen, born on December 16, 1775, in Steventon, Hampshire, England -died July 18, 1817, in Winchester, Hampshire, was an English author who profoundly shaped the modern novel by portraying the lives of ordinary people in everyday situations. Throughout her lifetime, she authored four novels: "Sense and Sensibility" (1811), "Pride and Prejudice" (1813), "Mansfield Park" (1814), and "Emma" (1815). In addition to these, "Persuasion" and "Northanger Abbey" were published posthumously in 1817. Through her vivid portrayals of early 19th-century English middle-class life, Austen not only established the novel of manners for her era but also created enduring classics that continued to be both critically acclaimed and popular for more than two centuries after her demise. Her literary works remain a testament to her lasting influence.

Fun Fact! Jane Austen is credited with popularizing the literary technique known as "free indirect discourse" or "free indirect style." While she didn't invent this style, she was among the early writers to consistently employ it in her works. Her use of this technique anticipated later authors like Virginia Woolf and James Joyce, who expanded upon it in early twentieth-century novels.

Holly Jackson

Holly Jackson was born in 1992. She grew up in Buckinghamshire and started writing stories from a young age, completing her first attempt at a novel aged fifteen. She graduated from the University of Nottingham with a 1st class degree and a master’s in English, focusing on literary linguistics and creative writing. She lives in London and her hobbies include binge-playing video games and pointing out grammatical errors in street signs.

Fun Fact! Holly Jackson has sold in over 25 languages, and has sold millions of copies both here and in the US, and around the world. A Good Girl’s Guide to Murder was a Sunday Times and NY Times no.1 bestseller and the series has spent over a year on the NYT bestseller list. It has won the National Book Award for Children's Book of the Year in 2020, Waterstones Children's Book of the Year, longlisted for the Branford Boase, and was a Waterstones Paperback of the Year. It was an Observer Book of the Year and WH Smith Thumping Good Read. It was published in the UK and in the US and has been translated in many other languages around the world.

Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe, born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts, -died October 7, 1849, in Baltimore, Maryland, was an American short-story writer, poet, critic, and editor renowned for his cultivation of mystery and the macabre. His tale "The Murders in the Rue Morgue" (1841) initiated the modern detective story, and the atmosphere in his tales of horror remains unrivaled in American fiction. His "The Raven" (1845) numbers among the best-known poems in the national literature.

Fun Fact! Edgar Allan Poe's prominent standing in world literature primarily stems from his brilliant and deep short stories, poems, and critical theories. He laid the foundation for a highly influential rationale for the short form in both poetry and fiction. In literary accounts and reference works, he is recognized as the mastermind behind the modern short story. Poe also played a pivotal role as the primary precursor of the "art for art's sake" movement in 19th-century European literature. While earlier critics often concentrated on moral or ideological aspects, Poe directed his critical attention toward the intricate details of style and construction that determined the success or failure of a literary work.

His writings are truly remarkable, and I highly encourage you to explore them! Here is a collection of some of the most notable quotes by Edgar Allan Poe:

- Eleonora, 1841

“Words have no power to impress the mind without the exquisite horror of their reality”

- The Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym of Nantucket, 1838

“There is no exquisite beauty… without some strangeness in the proportion”

- Ligeia, 1838

“Never to suffer would never to have been blessed”

- Mesmeric Revelation, 1849

“I became insane, with long intervals of horrible sanity.”

- a letter to George Washington Eveleth, 1846

Sylvia Plath

Sylvia Plath, born on October 27, 1932, in Boston, Massachusetts, U.S., and died on February 11, 1963, in London, England, was a highly influential American poet. Renowned for her distinctive voice, she is best known for powerful works such as the poems "Daddy" and "Lady Lazarus" and her novel, The Bell Jar. Plath's literary contributions are deeply rooted in her personal experiences, portraying a profound sense of alienation, self-destruction, and nature. Her writings, often marked by intense emotional exploration, provide a poignant reflection on the challenges faced by women in mid-20th-century America. Plath's impact extends beyond her literary achievements, making her a significant figure in the exploration of mental health and the female experience.

Fun Fact! At the tender age of eight, Plath unveiled her first poem to the public. Displaying her prodigious talent, she emerged victorious in numerous literary competitions. During her high school years, she achieved the remarkable feat of selling her maiden poem to The Christian Science Monitor and her inaugural short story to Seventeen magazine. Commencing her academic journey at Smith College in 1951, Plath further solidified her literary prowess by securing a co-victory in the 1952 Mademoiselle magazine fiction contest.

Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka, born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, Bohemia, Austria-Hungary (now in the Czech Republic) and died on June 3, 1924, in Kierling, near Vienna, Austria, was a German-language writer renowned for his visionary fiction. His significant works, notably the novel "Der Prozess" (1925; The Trial) and the story "Die Verwandlung" (1915; The Metamorphosis), eloquently articulate the anxieties and alienation experienced by many during the tumultuous 20th century in Europe and North America. Kafka's contributions have left an indelible mark on the literary landscape, exploring themes that resonate across time and cultures.

Fun Fact! Kafka's legacy is so influential that the term 'Kafkaesque' is widely employed to characterize surreal, uncanny situations or events reminiscent of his bizarre narratives. His native city, Prague, pays homage to him with two monuments and the Kafka Museum, established in 2005, devoted to his life and works. The city further honors Kafka's influence by presenting the Franz Kafka Prize annually, a prestigious award that not only includes a $10,000 prize but also confers a significant boost to the recipient's reputation.

His works are truly captivating, and I highly recommend them! Here, I've compiled some of the most exceptional quotes by Franz Kafka:
